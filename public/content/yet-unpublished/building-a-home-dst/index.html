Building a Home

Today, when a family wants to build a home to live in, there is an almost universal process with the following characteristics:

  • an architect acts as the professional intermediary between the family and the craftsmen;
  • a trained draftsman creates a number of aligned, precise and detailed blueprints;
  • the blueprints
    • are a visual model of the house to be built;
    • use a highly standardised visual language to avoid ambiguities and thus misunderstandigs among the parties;
    • are the source of truth for feasibility assessments, costs calculations, and for 95% of the craftmen's work.
  • the use of digital CAD tools increases the productivity and quality of drafting;

Building a Home like Software

The following domain-story diagram shows an (oversimplified) analogy to building by applying the methodology used in the typical digital transformation of an organisation.

Here, the characteristics are:

  • there is no professional intermediary — the family interacts directly with the craftsmen (and vice versa);
  • there are no up-front blueprints, i.e. no «big picture»;
  • each element of the house is negotiated, built and assessed individually — this is inefficient;
  • no truly digital toolsare used to boost productivit and quality (except for the actual software development);
  • eventually, the house is finished without there ever being a picture of the house before.

In summary:

  • at no point is there a coherent description of the house that is accessible to all parties, and on which the parties can aligin and agree;
  • misunderstandings and mistakes are inevitable;
  • if the house is to be extended or remodeled in the future, the parties will have to tackle each new need or design indivually and on the object itself;
  • anything invisible to the naked eye (water pipes, electrical cables, insulation, strength of beams and ceilings, etc.) is a risk to future changes.

Blueprints were introduced to building construction in the 15th century to improve both quality and productivity[3].
Before that, the architect and the craftsmen huddled every morning and discussed the works of the day.
Sounds familiar? Today, developer teams do it again and call it «daily standup» or «daily scrum».
